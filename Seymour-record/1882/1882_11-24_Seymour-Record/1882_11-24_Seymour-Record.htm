1882_11-24_Seymour-Record

 

OXFORD

 

Needed repairs have just been completed on the town buildings.

 

Irving, eldest son of S. P. Sanford, has recently commenced a term at the Parker Academy, Woodbury.

 

A busy appearance is presented at Towantic, a large number of men being employed in widening the rock cut preparatory for a double track.

 

Miss Lois E. Lum and Miss Mary E. Hubbell celebrated the anniversary of their birthday at the Oxford House last Tuesday evening. There was a good number of invited guests present, and the evening was mostly spent in dancing. The misses were the recipients of several presents.

 

SOUTHFORD

 

The workmen are busily engaged in putting up the machinery in the new paper mill.

 

The severity of the weather reminds us that winter is near at hand. We were visited with quite a snow last Friday .

 

Martin Stoddard, who has been living with Amelia Johnson on Hull's Hill, during the past summer, has returned to Woodbury.

 

Granville Warner of Hull's Hill, has recently made quite an improvement on his dwelling house by tearing out the old chimney, building a new one and making other repairs.

 

 

Note: The successful importation of Merino Sheep brought the first commercial/industrial success to Oxford,

with the added income to farmers and the development of mills for processing the wool.

 

GEN. DAVID HUMPHREYS.

BY CARLOS H. STORRS.

 

Gen. David Humphreys, of whom this town will ever be proud, and in whose honor it once bore the name of Hum-phreysville, which should ever have so remained in recognition of patriotic services to his country, was born in Derby, July 10th, 1752.

 

Being exceedingly fond of study, he prepared for college, and graduated at Yale in 1771. Having a poetical taste, during his college course he was designated as "the young bard of Yale." In the Revolution, not only by patriotic writings, but with efficient aid he promoted the cause of his country, and when, on the 19th of October, 1781, Lord Cornwallis surrendered at Yoiktown, Colonel Humphreys had the honor of receiving the English colors, in appreciation of which Congress voted him a sword, and in 1780, with imposing ceremonies, it was presented to him by Gen. Knox, then Secretary of War.

 

At the request of Washington lie accompanied him to his home in Virginia where he remained until 1784, when, on the appointment of Mr. Jefferson as Ambassador to France, he was appointed Secretary of Legation.

Returning to America in 1786, he revisited his native town, and for two consecutive years was chosen as its representative in the Legislature. Soon after he was appointed commander of a regiment raised for the Western service. Upon his regiment being reduced, in 1788, he retired to Mt. Vernon and remained with Washington uutil 1790, when he received the appointment of Minister to Portugal. He revisited America in 1704, and in 1797 was appointed Minister to Spain, which position he held until 1802.

 

It was during his residence in Spain that he carried into execution the project which so greatly benefited his country: namely, the importation of merino sheep into the United States.

 

Great was the expense and risk in importing these, for it was forbidden by stringent Spanish laws. However, he purchased four hundred sheep of the mountain shepherds; and they were driven to the coast during the night time, being secreted during the day. But their voyage was unfortunate, for when they were landed at the Derby docks, only one hundred remained. Gen. Humphreys did not do this as a speculation, yet it resulted ruinously to many honest and well meaning persoas. For soon the price of a merino buck went up to $1,000, and some even are reported to have been sold for more than twice that amount.

 

On the fifth and sixth of June, 1806, the frame of the first woolen factory in the United States was raised, which building is still standing on Factory street. Here he commenced the manufacture of broadcloth. Soon it became so famous that in November, 1808, Thomas Jefferson, who was then President of the United States, ordered enough for a suit of clothes, being desirous to appear at the White House on New Year's day in a suit of American manufacture. He carried on this manufacture until his death, which occurred Feb. 21st, 1818. His remains were interred in the New Haven cemetery.

 

Such, briefly, is the life of Gen. David Humphreys, the poet, the patriotic statesman, the friend of Washington, the companion of Jefferson, the Minister to Spain, the pioneer manufacturer of New England; a man whose character is worthy of imitation and whose memory will be cherished for generations to come.
