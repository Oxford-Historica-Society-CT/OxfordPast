OXFORD

After a vacation of three weeks, C. B. Perry returned on Wednesday to his studies at law school.

S. P. Sanford butchered a hog last Friday which weighted 615 lbs.

Rev. Mr. Snow will hold service at the Christian street school house next Sunday afternoon at 3.30 o'clock.

Rev. Mr. Snow's parishioners gave hi a donation visit on Tuesday afternoon and evening, the 10th inst., when he was the recipient of a good supply of the necessities of life.

The selectmen of Oxford and Monroe advertize to rent Zoar Bridge at 1- a.m. next Saturday for a term of three years from March 1st, 1882 the highest bidder.

Benjamin Nichols, Clerk E. Lum, and Orlando C. Oxborn are the superior Court Jurors from this town for the January criminal term.

The schools opened Monday with an increased attendance. Five pupils were promoted from the Center primary and twelve from the Bell school to the lower intermediate department of the High School, 14 from that room to the higher intermediate, 11 to the Junior Dept., and six to the Senior Dept. The numbers in several rooms are -- No 4, 47; No. 8, 40; No. 9, 45; No 10, 55; total 187, the largest number of pupils ever attending in what is known as the High School. Indeed it is pretty certain there were never before in town so many children of school age as now.

There was a good attendance at the lecture and entertainment last week Thursday evening. First on the programme was the music, which was well rendered by Miss Potwine of New Haven, Messrs. Langdale of New Haven, Green of Danbury, Hawley of Hawleyville, and Potwine. Rev. Mr. Robert's lecture on "Beaten Paths" was a subject well chosen and the delivery received high praise from all who had the pleasure to listen. Those who did not, missed an opportunity not often presented here. The ladies provided a bountiful spread in the dining room which was duly attended to after the exercises in the Hall. $25 was netted.

QUAKER FARMS

A man by the name of Bailey, a wandering coal burner, and apparently about 70 years old, died in a coal hut on Pisgah last week Tuesday night. Cause supposed to be pneumonia, brought on by exposure and want of proper care. Burial by the town.

William Hubbell, son of Samuel Hubbell, cut his foot quite badly while chopping in the woods last week.

Frank B. Thrall, who formerly worked for Mr. Clark Lum on Rock-house Hill, has gone to Philadelphia.

SOUTHFORD

The work of preparing the foundation for the new paper-mill about to be built by the Southford Manufacturing Co. is rapidly progressing. It is to be of brick and much larger than the old one which was burned a few months since.

Southford can now boast not only of a nice railroad station, but also of a coal, lumber and general storehouse recently built by Henry Wheeler, opposite the depot, a wagon and repair shop just completed by C. Bunnell, a blacksmith shop, a hotel, a store well stocked with choice groceries, dry goods and Yankee notions. Drugs and medicines can also be had here, and the customer who simply wants a clay pipe or a penny-worth of snuff will be just as cheerfully and pleasantly served as the one who has $25 to expend. In short, it is a good place to trade.

There is also a church where weekly religious service is held and a post-office in the place. We think we can predict growth, and if King Alcohol can be dethroned, future prosperity and happiness for Southford.
